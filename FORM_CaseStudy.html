Project Overview
What we set out to solve
A multidisciplinary research and design project exploring why people quit their fitness routines — and what a digital product can do to change that.
01
The Challenge
Most people start exercising with high motivation but quit within months. The gap between intention and long-term habit is the core design problem this project addresses.
02

We conducted qualitative interviews, a 30-person survey, interviewed a licensed personal trainer, and performed a competitive analysis across 4 major fitness apps.

02
The Problem
"How do we give people the motivation and discipline to incorporate training into their daily life — not just for a short period of time?"
01
Lack of Motivation
Results aren't visible immediately. When the effort doesn't match perceived progress, motivation fades. People need tangible, measurable feedback to stay engaged.
02
Time Constraints
Busy schedules, work, family and social commitments make it difficult to protect time for training. Without a plan, training is always the first thing to get cut.
03
Knowledge Gap
Without understanding proper technique, programming, or progressive overload, people jump between exercises at random — getting no real results, and risking injury.
04
Life Disruption
Illness, travel, or family emergencies break routines. Without systems to re-engage, a temporary break becomes a permanent quit.
Core Insight
Fitness apps already exist. The problem isn't access to workout information — it's the absence of habit architecture that keeps people showing up consistently over months and years.

Research
Listening before designing
We spoke to 20+ people aged 18–58 across school, work, and the public. We also surveyed 30 users online and interviewed a licensed personal trainer.
📋
Online Survey — 30 Respondents
Collected via Google Forms. Targeted age 18–58. Covered motivation levels, exercise habits, what frustrates them, and what would help them stay consistent.
🎙️
Qualitative Interviews — 20+ People
Face-to-face and remote interviews with students, professionals, parents, and strangers. Identified patterns in quitting behaviour and what people actually want from a fitness product.

Exercise knowledge is the missing piece

04
Key Findings
What the data revealed
📉
High expectations, fast burnout
Users expect visible results within weeks. When that doesn't happen, motivation collapses. The app must recalibrate expectations and celebrate micro-progress early.
⏰
Time is the enemy
80%+ of interviewees cited time as a primary barrier. Short, high-intensity sessions (supersets, 30-min workouts) need to be first-class citizens in the product.
🧠
Technique is invisible but critical
People train without knowing what muscle they're targeting. The PT confirmed that technique and structure — not volume — are the biggest determinants of results for beginners.
👥
Community creates accountability
A fitness community was rated highly — but not for competition. Users want solidarity and shared experience, not leaderboards that highlight inequality in starting points.
🏆
Rewards need careful design
The PT warned against badly designed reward systems. Points and discounts can create manic behavior. The best reward is seeing your own data — statistics, before/after photos, personal bests.
📈
Progress tracking is the killer feature
Stats-based progress tracking was universally valued across all personas. The PT himself uses one. Seeing your development curve over time builds intrinsic motivation — the only kind that lasts.

PT Expert Insights
Straight from a professional
We asked a licensed personal trainer 8 direct questions. These answers shaped our product decisions.
On why people quit within months
"People don't plan their everyday life correctly to make time for training. They want results fast, and when they don't appear, motivation dies out."
On creating discipline
"Discipline is created by setting a goal you can actually achieve, and a training schedule that creates structure in your life."
On why people don't see results
"Lack of technique, lack of nutritional knowledge, and lack of structure. People jump between exercises without knowing which muscle they're training."
On training with little time
"If you're short on time you must do a more intensive session. Supersets — training two antagonist muscle groups back to back — are ideal."
On reward systems
"Exercise itself is a reward — it secretes endorphins. It depends on the system. If done right, there can be benefits. But it can also create manic behavior."
On before/after photo features
"It can definitely be motivating — but only if the person has actually gotten results. If they look the same after three months, it can be very unmotivating without support."
On progress statistics in apps
"I use such an app myself. It's very good. It helps you track training and understand your development curve."
On community features
"A community creates unity. Many need that feeling to maintain motivation. But don't compare weights and reps — everyone's starting point is too different."
08
The Solution
A fitness app built on habit science
"To create a training app that gives the right tools to show people how to train, and builds a community that gives people a feeling of comfort."
Our solution combines structured programming, intelligent progress tracking, technique education, and a supportive community — without the overwhelm of apps that try to do everything at once.
📋
Structured Training Plans
Curated programs matched to user goals and experience. Not a gym with 500 machines — a personal coach with a plan. Weekly structure visible at a glance.
📊
Progress Statistics Dashboard
Visual charts for weights, reps, streaks, personal bests. The data makes progress concrete — especially in the early weeks when the mirror hasn't caught up yet.
🎥
Technique Library
Video and written guidance for every exercise — filling the gap no competitor has filled. Beginners understand what they're doing. Advanced users refine form.
👥
Community (Without Competition)
A social feed for sharing wins, tips, and motivation — not leaderboards. The goal is solidarity, not comparison. Community members celebrate each other's personal progress.
🔥
Streak & Habit System
Daily and weekly streaks tied to scheduled workouts. Miss a session? Re-engage flow brings you back gently. The product removes friction from returning after a break.
⚡
Time-Optimised Workouts
Superset-based 25–40 min sessions for users who only have limited gym time. Research-backed intensity over duration. No excuse of "I don't have 2 hours."
09
Outcome & Learnings
What this project delivered
HABIT FIRST
The single design principle that emerged from every research conversation. The app doesn't just track workouts — it builds the architecture of showing up.
User Research Product Strategy Feature Definition Competitive Analysis Persona Design
01
Research changes everything
We assumed a reward point system would be popular. The data — and the PT expert — strongly cautioned against it. Without the research, we'd have built the wrong feature.
02
Simplicity is a product decision
Competitors like Fiton failed not because they lacked features — but because they had too many. The hardest UX decision is what to leave out. Focus beats breadth every time.
03
The blank screen is the enemy
Both personas were stopped by not knowing what to do when they arrived at the gym. Onboarding and first-session guidance are not secondary features — they are the product.
